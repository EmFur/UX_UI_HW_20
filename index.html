SPLITz Mobile App Challenge

My Role: UX Designer

The Team: Jessica Allred, Em Furukawa

THE PROJECT

The Problem

When group expenses arise during trips with friends or family, travelers are often forced to utilize multiple tools to record, split, charge, and pay group members. This can easily distract and disturb travelers from enjoying the time being spent with the ones they love.

The Solution

SPLITz is a travel assistance tool that allows travelers to take care of their group expenses all in one app. record, split, charge, and pay your trip members in one app without wasting time going through multiple apps.

Project Overview

In this project, the team was tasked to come up with an idea for a travel assistance app that will help travelers as restrictions begin lifting post-pandemic. I was assigned to establish the onboarding flow of the app, going from the user opening the app, logging in or creating an account, and ending on the user dash. The team collaborated on the user research, defining the problem, and coming up with the solution, but then we split ways to each iterate wireframes and prototypes of our respective task flows.

Responsibilities

User research plan, user interviews, collaborate on creating proto & user personas, develop user insights, competitor analysis, storyboarding, wireframing, prototyping, create hi-fi mockups of the onboarding flow, design app logo

Timeline & Scope

Three Weeks to pitch an idea, perform research, define & ideate, prototype, and deliver hi-fidelity mockups.

USER RESEARCH

User Survey

Before beginning our user research process, we conducted a short survey involving 24 individuals to get an idea of how our users currently deal with IOUs that happen during a trip. The top responses stated that most people 'manually notates for later' or 'Immediately request funds through apps'.

User Interviews

The goal of this study is to find out how people keep teack of expenses and IOUs when traveling with a group, as well as their chosen/go-to method. We conducted 4-6 interviews in-person, over the phone, and/or on Zoom. There was a trend in our users that claimed they prefer not to be the one planning the group trips when they happen because of the complexities money brings into the process.

“I usually end up being the organizer but I personally don’t want to because it's a lot of work. People can be super territorial over their money and things can get really sticky really quick. ”

“I don’t plan trips as a group. I just invite people to trips I’m going to anyways. If they can’t afford it, they don’t have to come. I like to be in charge but not for other peoples budgets too.”

DEFINITION & SYNTHESIS

Meet Jay!

Jay is a single 28 year-old male who lives in New Jersey, working as an Executive Assistant. He loves to hang out with his friends, and is especially excited by the idea of going to trips with them. However, he hates the pressure of owing people things, and easily gets frustrated when people aren't on the same page, which happend quite often when he would plan long vacations with his friends. He wants to enjoy a trip without worrying about money, and therefore desires new methods help stay more organized.

User Storyboard
Identifying the Problem

We have observed that existing products and methods aren't able to streamline the process of recording, requesting, and paying back split expenses, which is causing travelers to use multiple tools to accomplish these often necessary tasks. This may disturb them from enjoying the vacation experience and cause an increase in stressors that are associated with money during a trip.

IDEATION

After defining the problem, I proceeded to the ideation phase in which I tried to come up with a solution that will alleviate this pain point. The team went through an activity called the "I like, I wish, What if" to come up with some plausible feature we like in existing travel finance applications, some features we wish to see, and some that are impossible now, but maybe in the future.

We then organized these ideas in a Feature Priortization Matrix to focus on solutions that we can realisitcally implement into our product.

Ideating on a solution then helped us to establish our value proposition:

SPLITz will assist travelers to record, split, charge, and pay group travel expenses that happen during the trip all within one app - no more fumbling your phone alone while your family and friends enjoy the vacation.

USER TESTING & OUTCOMES

The Mid-Fi prototype was put through a user test consisting of three tasks to test its functionality and intuitivity:

  1. Create a new group and add members to it immediately ollowing the sign-up process.
  2. The user understands the difference betweeen the home page and the group dash
  3. The user is able to link an account (Venmo, Paypal, Bank or Credit) to their SPLITz account.
Key Learnings
  • The icon in the top-left navigation bar should stay consistent. Some users were thrown off by the change in icons (menu vs home), which thwarted them from smoothly navigating between the Home Page and the Group Dashboard.
  • Each feature (for this test, it was the Linked Accounts page) should have another button or an access point other than the one in the menu. One suggested that we add widgets to the home page below the groups list as another visual que.
  • Offer an option to Link an Account in the onboarding process so that the user will know that it's a feature and an option that is offered on this product.

FUTURE OPPORTUNITIES

Final Thoughts & Conclusion

1

Going into the mind of the user. Especially at the beginning, it was very difficult for me to truly grasp what is important when brainstorming user-focused ideas. I got too hung up on all the amazing ideas that I was having for the app, that I was not truly trying to align with the user's needs. Now, I understand that the beginning brainstorms are especially important to build the foundation of user-friendly service and features.

2

Do what works for me. When going through this project, I found that there are many different tools that can be used to perform the same tasks, and different people preferred different things. It is important to have an understanding of how to use the tools your team members are using, but at the end of the day, I should use the tools that I am most comfortable with to produce the highest quality work.

3

Research your Competitors. Nothing I design will truly be original - so reference everything that you see. The patterns and functions that you see in your competitors are there for a reason. Everyone has similar things because it works. Research them and don't be afraid to implement them into your own work (without plagiarizing, of course).
